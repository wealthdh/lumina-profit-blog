How Polymarket Works: The Mechanics

Understanding Market Structure

Every Polymarket consists of two contracts: YES and NO. If an event is "Will Bitcoin exceed $50,000 by December 31, 2026?":

  • YES contract: Pays $1 if Bitcoin hits $50K
  • NO contract: Pays $1 if Bitcoin doesn't

If YES is trading at $0.65, it means the market assigns a 65% probability to Bitcoin hitting $50K. NO would be trading at $0.35 (they always sum to $1).

Pricing Mechanism

Polymarket uses an Automated Market Maker (AMM) to set prices. The formula accounts for how much liquidity exists on each side. When you buy YES contracts, you're moving the price up in real-time based on supply/demand.

Settlement

When an event resolves, winning contracts are automatically paid out. The smart contract reads the outcome from an oracle (typically UMA for Polymarket), settles trades, and funds appear in your wallet within hours.

Understanding Market Types on Polymarket

1. Binary Markets (Largest Volume)

Example: "Will Trump win the 2028 election?"

Simple YES/NO structure. Most liquid markets. Best for traders focused on one prediction. Current market: YES at $0.58 (58% probability Trump wins).

2. Categorical Markets (Growing Fastest)

Example: "Which party will win the 2026 UK general election? (Conservatives / Labour / Lib Dem / Other)"

Multiple outcome options. More complex analysis needed. Higher potential payoffs if you're the only one with the right forecast.

3. Scalar Markets (Advanced)

Example: "What will the average Bitcoin price be in Q4 2026?" (Range: $30K - $100K)

These allow precise predictions on magnitude. Profitable but require more sophisticated analysis.

Profitable Trading Strategies for Prediction Markets

Strategy #1: Information Advantage Trading

The best traders have access to non-public information sources faster than the market. Examples:

When a market overreacts to news (fear or euphoria), the price becomes inefficient. Professional traders identify these moments and trade against the crowd.

Research Framework: How to Win Predictions

Step 1: Understand Base Rates

What's the historical frequency of this outcome? If you're predicting "Will a major terrorist attack occur in 2026?", the base rate is roughly 8-15% globally per year based on historical data.

Step 2: Identify Relevant Factors

For each market, list the 3-5 most important variables:

Example - "Will Bitcoin hit $100K by Dec 31, 2026?"

  • Current price and historical volatility
  • Regulatory environment changes
  • Macroeconomic conditions (Fed policy, inflation)
  • Institutional adoption trends
  • Technological breakthroughs or failures

Step 3: Model the Probability

Assign rough probabilities to scenarios. Bayesian thinking is key: start with base rates, then update based on new information.

Step 4: Compare to Market Price

If your model says 40% probability but market says 60%, you have a trade. Buy if underpriced, sell if overpriced. Aim for 15%+ edge before committing capital.

Step 5: Monitor and Update

Track developments weekly. Update your probability as new information arrives. Disciplined traders adjust their positions as they learn more.

Risk Management for Prediction Markets

Prediction markets can feel "safer" than leveraged trading because you know your max loss (the amount you invested). But portfolio-level risk management is still critical.

Position Sizing

Allocate capital by confidence level: High confidence positions get 5% of bankroll, medium 3%, low 1%. This prevents one bad prediction from destroying your portfolio.

Diversification Across Markets

Don't put all capital in one event type. Spread across:

  • Political events (3-4 year timeframes)
  • Macroeconomic outcomes (1-2 years)
  • Sports/celebrity events (6-12 months)
  • Crypto and technology predictions (3-12 months)

Take Profits Early

If you buy YES at $0.30 and it hits $0.50 (66% gain), don't wait for it to hit $1.00. Lock in 50%, let the rest ride. This protects edge.

Avoid "Gambler's Ruin"

Never bet more than 2% of total capital on single events. Even skilled predictors are wrong sometimes. Proper position sizing lets you survive losing streaks.
